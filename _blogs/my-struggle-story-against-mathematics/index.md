There is a particular kind of shame that only math can produce in you. It's not like failing an essay, where you can at least tell yourself the grader didn't *understand* your point. With math, the answer is either right or wrong. The red pen doesn't lie.

For most of my secondary school years, I was on the wrong side of that red pen — a lot.

---

## Where It Started

I don't think I was born bad at math. Looking back, I think I was bad at *sitting still long enough to understand math*. I was the kind of student who would copy down the formula, memorize the steps, pass the quiz, and then forget everything a week later because I never actually understood *why* any of it worked.

This strategy held up through basic arithmetic. It held up through early algebra. It even held up, barely, through the first year of high school.

Then I met calculus.

Calculus doesn't let you fake it. The concepts build on each other so tightly that if you don't understand limits, derivatives make no sense. If derivatives don't make sense, integrals are just a ritual you perform without meaning. I was performing the ritual. I had no idea what I was doing.

My first calculus exam, I scored 38 out of 100. I stared at that number for a long time.

---

## The Low Point

What made it worse was that I *wanted* to understand. It wasn't that I didn't care. I cared a lot — maybe too much. I'd sit in class, listen to the teacher explain something, nod along like I was following, and then go home and open my notebook and realize I had retained almost nothing.

I started avoiding math class. Not physically — I still showed up — but mentally. I'd zone out. I told myself I was just "not a math person." That phrase is so dangerous. It lets you off the hook so comfortably.

I remember one night, maybe 11 PM, sitting at my desk with a practice problem set. My brother — who was already preparing his applications to universities abroad — walked past my room, glanced at what I was doing, and just said: *"You need to stop trying to memorize and start trying to understand."*

He didn't say it to be harsh. He was right. I just wasn't ready to hear it yet.

---

## The Turn

The turn came slowly, and it didn't come from school.

It came from a YouTube channel — one of those channels where someone draws everything on a blackboard and actually explains *why* the chain rule is the way it is, not just *how* to apply it. I watched one video. Then another. Then I went back to my textbook and things started to click in a way they never had before.

I started asking a different question. Instead of *"what do I do?"*, I started asking *"what is this actually saying?"*

A derivative isn't a formula. It's a description of how fast something is changing. An integral isn't a procedure. It's an accumulation — you're adding up infinitely many infinitely small things. Once I understood that, the formulas stopped feeling like arbitrary rules and started feeling like *descriptions of reality*.

That shift — from *memorization* to *understanding* — changed everything.

---

## Where I Am Now

I'm not going to pretend I suddenly became a math prodigy. I didn't. I still make calculation errors. I still have to look up formulas sometimes. Calculus 3 is genuinely hard, and there are moments in class where I feel that old familiar fog rolling in.

But now I know what to do when the fog comes. I slow down. I ask what the concept is *actually saying*. I draw pictures. I find a different explanation.

Studying Data Science has been the most satisfying thing I've done, partly because it forced me to finally make peace with mathematics. Linear algebra, probability, statistics, optimization — they all live inside math. And I'm not afraid of them anymore.

---

## What I'd Tell My Younger Self

If I could go back and tell the kid staring at that 38/100 anything, it would be this:

> **You are not bad at math. You are bad at the way you've been taught to approach math. That's fixable.**

The "math person" myth is one of the most harmful things we tell students. Mathematical thinking is a skill, not a talent. It can be learned. It just requires a different kind of patience than most school environments teach you — the patience to sit with confusion long enough to dissolve it, rather than rushing to an answer you'll forget by Friday.

I'm still learning that patience. But at least now I know it's worth learning.
